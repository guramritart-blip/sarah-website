Bottom line

The California Energy Commission is being asked to approve a battery plant that two local governments have already banned, on prime farmland, next to homes, and to erase every environmental problem except the view by calling it "mitigated."

The single strongest attack is the alternatives analysis: the state concedes in writing that a nearby industrial site would be zoning-compliant, farther from homes, and would save the farmland, then rejects it on a distance figure the developer supplied. The second is deferred mitigation: nearly every "less than significant" finding rests on a plan that will be written after approval. Both are the kind of defects that get certifications sent back.

Part I
The project and the play

01Executive summary

A subsidiary of NextEra Energy Resources, the largest renewable-energy developer in the United States, wants to build a 300-megawatt, 1,200-megawatt-hour lithium-ion battery installation on a 40-acre former orchard in unincorporated Solano County, a mile from PG&E's Vaca-Dixon substation.

It cannot get a permit locally. In November 2025 Solano County banned this exact use in agricultural zones, and in March 2026 the City of Vacaville, which the transmission line crosses, banned lithium-ion battery storage by name because of fire risk. So the developer routed around local government entirely, applying to the CEC under the state's Opt-In Certification Program, which lets the Commission issue a certificate that supersedes local law.

The Staff Assessment then evaluates sixteen environmental subject areas and concludes that fifteen of them are "less than significant," most only after the applicant agrees to conditions of certification. The lone exception is visual impact, which staff call significant and unavoidable and propose to approve anyway through a "statement of overriding considerations."

This brief argues that the "less than significant" conclusions are, in many places, labels rather than findings, that the alternatives analysis is inadequate on its face, and that the override rests on a thin benefits recital. It then lays out exactly how Blessin can put those arguments on the administrative record, where they count.

03The legal machine: the Opt-In program

Know the rules of the room

You cannot fight this well without understanding the procedure the developer chose, because the procedure is the point.

In 2022, California created an Opt-In Certification Program (Public Resources Code §25545 et seq.) that lets developers of certain clean-energy projects bypass city and county permitting and take their application straight to the CEC. If the Commission certifies the project, that certificate supersedes local law, PRC §25545.1(b)(1), and the CEC's environmental review under CEQA takes the place of the local process.

The trade-off the Legislature built in: the CEC's Staff Assessment is a draft Environmental Impact Report, so it must satisfy the same CEQA standards a county EIR would. That is the leverage. The developer escaped the county's zoning ban, but it did not escape CEQA. Every CEQA doctrine, adequate alternatives, no deferred mitigation, substantial evidence for every significance finding, a legitimate override, applies here with full force. This brief is organized around those doctrines because that is where an Opt-In certification is legally vulnerable.

Why this matters strategically

Opt-In runs on a compressed statutory clock. That speed is a feature for the developer and a trap for opponents who wait. The window to build the administrative record, comments, expert declarations, hearing testimony, is short and closes hard. Getting organized now is worth more than a perfect brief filed late.

Part II
The vulnerabilities

04Two local bans, overridden

CriticalPolitical + Legal

The community did not fail to object. It already said no, twice, through its elected governments.

  • Solano County, Ordinance ZT-25-02 (Nov 4, 2025). Amended the zoning code to prohibit front-of-the-meter BESS (1 MW+) in Agricultural, Rural Residential, Residential-Traditional Community, Commercial, Watershed & Conservation, Marsh Preservation, and Park zones. It confined BESS to Manufacturing/Industrial zones by use permit. The Corby parcel is zoned A-40 Agricultural. §5.8
  • City of Vacaville BESS ordinance (adopted Mar 10, 2026). Prohibits front-of-the-meter BESS "technologies that contain lithium-ion or other technology types with documented occurrences of thermal runaway events." Corby is lithium-ion. The gen-tie runs through the city. §5.8

The SA's response is to invoke the supersession clause and move on, characterizing the conflict as the project simply "would not comply" with the county ordinance, an inconsistency the certificate cures. It never engages the substance of Vacaville's finding, that this battery chemistry is dangerous enough to ban, against its own conclusion that fire risk is "less than significant."

What they're covering up

A local legislative body's determination of danger is evidence, and CEQA findings must rest on substantial evidence in the whole record. Vacaville looked at this exact technology and concluded, as law, that its thermal-runaway record makes it unsafe here. The SA does not rebut that with data, it routes around it with jurisdiction. That gap, an unrebutted contrary finding by the most local expert body, is exactly the kind of thing that undermines a "substantial evidence" defense of the fire conclusion.

Kill-shot question

"Two elected governments prohibited this facility here, and Vacaville banned this battery chemistry by name because of documented fires. What substantial evidence in the record rebuts those local findings, as opposed to merely superseding them by statute?"

05The alternatives shell game

CriticalStrongest CEQA attack

CEQA's core command: if a feasible alternative would meet the project's goals with fewer significant impacts, the agency must seriously evaluate it and cannot approve the worse option without a defensible reason. Here the document convicts itself.

The public and the Board of Supervisors both demanded analysis of Lambie Industrial Park, a site actually zoned for this use. Staff's own conclusion: §8.9

"The land use and zoning designations for this alternative site are compatible with a BESS facility… the site would be located at a greater distance from sensitive receptors than the proposed project site. Construction of the BESS facility at the Lambie Industrial Park would also avoid the conversion of Important Farmland." — SA §8.9, Environmentally Superior Alternative

That is a written admission that the alternative is better on three of the project's biggest problems: local-zoning compliance, distance from homes, and farmland. So how is it rejected? On a single factor, a 14-mile gen-tie to reach the substation, and the reasoning is riddled with holes:

  • The 14-mile figure and the alternative's feasibility come from the applicant's own data responses, the party that wants it rejected. §8.7
  • Staff concede the alternative was "evaluated in less detail" and that they "made necessary assumptions." CEQA does not permit rejecting the environmentally superior alternative on a thinner analysis than the preferred project got. §8.9
  • The applicant already owns the Montezuma Wind Energy Center adjacent to Lambie, a fact staff themselves flag, which suggests interconnection paths the 14-mile assumption ignores. §8.7
  • Every gen-tie route alternative was dismissed with the identical boilerplate, that it "would not lessen or avoid any of the impacts," rather than a route-specific analysis. §8.7.14–8.7.16
  • A Reduced Project (175 MW / 700 MWh, dropping 126 enclosures) would move the batteries from 880 ft to ~1,140 ft from the nearest home. Staff evaluated and set it aside. §8.7.17

The pattern is a rigged comparison set: every "alternative site" the applicant volunteered is also on Important Farmland next to residences, so of course none looks better, while the one genuinely superior industrial site is knocked out on a self-supplied number.

Kill-shot question

"Staff concede Lambie is zoning-compliant, farther from homes, and avoids farmland loss, then reject it on a 14-mile gen-tie figure the applicant supplied, in an analysis staff admit was less detailed than the preferred project's. Where is the independent engineering study of interconnection from the applicant's own adjacent Montezuma site, and how does rejecting the environmentally superior alternative on a thinner record satisfy CEQA?"

06Fire and toxic gas

CriticalPublic safety

The SA states the danger in its own words, then makes it vanish with two condition numbers.

"Thermal runaway is a process in which the lithium-ion cell enters an uncontrollable, self-heating state and can emit toxic gases such as hydrogen chloride, hydrogen fluoride, hydrogen cyanide, and benzene… staff conclude that the project's BESS would present a significant risk that should be mitigated." — SA §5.7 (Hazards, Hazardous Materials, and Wildfire)

Hydrogen fluoride and hydrogen cyanide, 880 feet from a bedroom. Staff label it a "significant risk", then reduce it to "less than significant" via WORKER SAFETY-7 and -8, which amount to training the fire department and preparing a fire-prevention plan. The physical hazard is unchanged; only the adjective moves. Four soft spots:

  • The school question is answered with a ruler. CEQA asks about hazards within a quarter-mile of a school. Nearest school is ~1.3 miles from the gen-tie, so the box is checked "No Impact." But a fire plume travels on wind, not in a quarter-mile circle, which is why the SA's own Appendix D models dispersion for miles. The screening distance and the physical reality point in opposite directions. §5.7(c) · App. D
  • The reassurance predates the worst-case event. Staff rely on a NYSERDA 2023 study to say fire byproducts "did not pose a significant concern." That is from before the January 2025 Moss Landing BESS fire, the most serious in California history. Using pre-Moss-Landing evidence to dismiss post-Moss-Landing fear is directly quotable. §5.7
  • The first responder is a rural fire department. The plan leans on the Dixon Fire Department, equipped via COCs with gas detectors and a "toxic chemical detecting drone." A volunteer-scale department is being asked to manage a multi-million-pound lithium fire, a capacity question worth pressing on the record.
  • Firewater becomes hazardous waste. The SA concedes water used to fight the fire "may come into contact with the smoke plume… resulting in the water mixing with hazardous materials." The response (HAZ-9) is to sample it after, not to contain it before it moves. See §7. §5.7
Kill-shot question

"Staff's own text calls the fire risk 'significant.' The measures cited to reduce it are training and planning documents, not a physical barrier or greater setback. Point to the substantial evidence that a training plan reduces hydrogen-fluoride exposure at a home 880 feet away to 'less than significant,' and explain the reliance on a 2023 study that predates Moss Landing."

07Water and wildlife

CriticalBlessin's core mission

This is Blessin's terrain, and the SA handles all of it with one move: assert that standard procedures will prevent the harm, then declare it less than significant.

The frac-out. The transmission line will be bored under the Solano Irrigation District canal by horizontal directional drilling. The SA concedes the risk directly: §5.2

"During HDD activities there is a possibility that drilling mud could escape and enter the canal in what is described as a frac-out… Frac-outs can result in adverse impacts to aquatic resources should they occur within a waterbody." — SA §5.2 (Biological Resources)

The referee joins one team. The fix is COC BIO-19, a "Lake and Streambed Equivalency." Rather than the developer obtaining an independent Lake and Streambed Alteration Agreement from the Department of Fish and Wildlife under Fish & Game Code §1602, the CEC issues its own "in-lieu" version. The independent wildlife-agency permit is replaced by the agency approving the project, and CDFW's role drops to a consultation. §5.2

Shallow groundwater, reactive chemistry. Groundwater sits just 7 to 15 feet below the surface, and the basin (Solano subbasin) is a medium-priority basin under a state Sustainable Groundwater Management Act plan. Lithium iron phosphate is expressly listed as reactive with water. Firewater contaminated with battery byproducts, plus particulate settling into shallow soils, is a direct pathway to both the canal and the aquifer, and the mitigation (HAZ-9) is post-hoc sampling, not prevention. §5.16 · §5.7

The disappearing creek. The route also touches seasonal wetlands and a segment of Gibson Canyon Creek. The SA repeatedly downgrades them, "highly modified," "straightened," "not a sensitive natural community", to justify minimal protection. That framing is a judgment call, not a fact, and an independent biologist should test it. The site also hosts state-listed and protected wildlife, Swainson's hawk (threatened), golden eagle, white-tailed kite, burrowing owl, American badger, Crotch's bumble bee, monarch, all "mitigated" to less than significant. §5.2

What they're covering up

There is no analysis anywhere of what a contaminated firewater release into the SID canal would do to downstream irrigation users or aquatic life, the precise harm Blessin exists to prevent. The document analyzes the canal as a construction obstacle (can we drill under it safely?) but never as a living water system with users below it.

Kill-shot question

"Groundwater here is 7 to 15 feet down, the SID canal carries live irrigation flows, and the battery chemistry is reactive with water. Where is the analysis of downstream impacts to irrigation users and aquatic species from a contaminated firewater release, and why is the CEC self-issuing a streambed 'equivalency' instead of requiring an independent CDFW §1602 agreement?"

08Prime farmland

High

The SA concedes the permanent conversion of 40.3 acres of DOC-designated Important Farmland is a significant impact, then "mitigates" it by requiring the developer to buy an agricultural conservation easement elsewhere in the county. §5.8

An easement on other land grows no food on this land. It restricts future development somewhere else; the orchard here is still gone permanently. Two local protections are also being overridden: the County's Agricultural Mitigation Program, and Vacaville's Ordinance No. 1942, which requires 1:1 farmland-and-avian-habitat mitigation for exactly this kind of conversion. The Opt-In certificate rolls over both. And because the SA's cumulative list (see §13) includes numerous other Solano projects converting farmland, the regional loss is a legitimate cumulative-impact argument the document underplays. §5.8 · App. A

Kill-shot question

"An off-site easement preserves land that produces zero crops to replace what's lost here. How is buying development rights elsewhere 'mitigation' for permanently destroying this Important Farmland, and where is the cumulative farmland-loss analysis across all pending Solano BESS and development projects?"

09Public health and the modeling

HighProbe the assumptions

The routine-operations health analysis concludes impacts are below the significance thresholds, cancer risk under 10-in-a-million and a non-cancer hazard index under 1.0. That conclusion is only as good as its inputs, and the inputs deserve scrutiny. §5.10

  • It models the normal day, not the bad day. The health risk assessment addresses routine construction and operation emissions. The catastrophic scenario, a thermal-runaway fire venting hydrogen fluoride and cyanide over the neighbors, is shunted to the Hazards section and Appendix D, where it is treated as "rare" rather than as a public-health impact to be mitigated. The worst exposure the neighbors actually fear is not in the public-health significance finding at all.
  • Screening-level, by the SA's own admission. Staff note the analysis is "designed to overstate actual risks", presented as conservative, but it also means a refined model (HARP2) was not necessarily run to ground for every receptor. Ask for the refined assessment for the maximally exposed resident at 880 ft.
  • The nearest sensitive receptors are close and specific. A residence 160 ft from the boundary, Kaiser Permanente Vacaville 1.25 miles west, schools within ~1.7 miles. These are real, named, exposed populations, not abstractions. §5.10
Kill-shot question

"The public-health finding models routine emissions and sends the fire scenario elsewhere as 'rare.' Where is the health-risk analysis of a thermal-runaway plume for the maximally exposed resident 880 feet away, and was a refined (HARP2) assessment, not just the screening model, completed for that receptor?"

10Noise, 24 hours a day

High

A BESS is not silent. Its cooling systems, the HVAC and inverter fans, run continuously, including all night. Measured nighttime ambient in this rural area is only about 56 to 62 dBA, quiet, and rural nights are quiet precisely because there is nothing industrial nearby. A permanent 24/7 mechanical hum 880 ft from homes is a real and permanent change in the living environment, and low-frequency tonal noise from banks of fans is exactly the kind that carries and annoys. §5.9

Press two things: whether the operational-noise modeling uses the quiet nighttime ambient as its baseline (as it should) rather than a daytime average that hides the change, and whether any tonal/low-frequency penalty was applied. Noise is a category where a specific expert measurement can beat a modeled assurance.

11Air, GHG, and the super-pollutant

Credibility of the "clean" claim

Construction emissions of criteria pollutants are found less than significant only "with mitigation" (COC AQ-SC1 and others), i.e., another mitigation-dependent finding to test. §5.1

More useful rhetorically: the entire override is justified by climate benefit, yet the facility stores 500 lb of sulfur hexafluoride (SF₆), a greenhouse gas with a 100-year global-warming potential of 22,800× CO₂, in its breakers, plus HFC-32 and other F-gas refrigerants. It is fair to note on the record that a project sold as climate salvation warehouses one of the most potent greenhouse gases known, on retired farmland, over the community's objection. §5.3 · Table 5.7-1

12Decommissioning and the shell company

HighFollow the money

Quiet, rarely raised, and therefore effective.

The applicant is Corby Energy Storage, LLC, a single-purpose subsidiary. In roughly 30 years the site must be decommissioned: nine million pounds of batteries, 39,000 gallons of mineral oil, SF₆, and toxic residue removed, and the land restored. The "Facility Closure" section describes this aspirationally, materials "anticipated to be recycled," restoration "in accordance with an approved decommissioning plan." §3.10

What is absent is the only thing that guarantees it happens: a funded financial assurance, a decommissioning bond, surety, or trust sized to the real cost and adjusted for inflation. A review of the assessment turns up electrical "bonding" and salvage aspirations, but no posted security. If the single-purpose LLC is dissolved or insolvent in 2056, the cleanup lands on the landowner, the county, or the public.

What they're covering up

The limited-liability structure exists so the parent can walk away. Paired with an unfunded decommissioning promise, that is the standard recipe for an abandoned industrial site. Demanding a bonded, inflation-indexed decommissioning fund as a condition of approval is reasonable, specific, and the applicant will resist it, and the resistance is itself the argument.

Kill-shot question

"What legally binding, funded financial assurance guarantees the full cost of battery removal, oil and SF₆ disposal, and site restoration will be paid in ~30 years if Corby Energy Storage, LLC no longer exists? If there is none, how is decommissioning 'less than significant'?"

13Cumulative impacts

HighUnderplayed in the SA

CEQA requires an agency to consider a project's impacts together with other past, present, and reasonably foreseeable projects. This region is about to change enormously, and Corby is one piece of a very large picture.

The SA's own cumulative list (Appendix A) and the surrounding record include: App. A

  • Another battery project next door. A 57 MW / 57 MWh + 100 MW / 400 MWh BESS on APN 0133-060-060 is listed as "under review", a second large battery installation in the immediate area, compounding the fire, visual, and hazard profile Corby creates.
  • California Forever. The controversial proposal to build an entirely new city in Solano County appears in the cumulative references, a regional-scale land-use transformation.
  • Thousands of housing units. Nearby specific plans and developments (Lagoon Valley, Peabody-Allison, and parcels showing 969 and 2,931 units) plus warehouses and the Vaca-Dixon Power Center, converting farmland and adding population and traffic in the same airshed and watershed.

Individually each impact is called "less than significant." The cumulative question is whether Corby's contribution is cumulatively considerable when stacked on a second BESS, a new city, and thousands of homes, on fire risk, farmland loss, traffic, water demand, and visual character together. This is a place where the SA's category-by-category "less than significant" framing is most vulnerable, because harm that is individually small can be cumulatively decisive, and courts scrutinize thin cumulative analyses.

Kill-shot question

"With a second large BESS under review nearby, the California Forever proposal, and thousands of planned housing units in the same region, where is the cumulative analysis of concentrated battery-fire risk, farmland loss, and water demand, and on what basis is Corby's contribution not 'cumulatively considerable'?"

14Visual — the one they concede

Their admitted weak point

Staff conclude visual impact is significant and unavoidable at six key observation points (KOP 2a, 2b, 3, 5a, 5b, 5c), the project "would substantially degrade the existing visual character or quality of public views", creating "a new industrial footprint within a rural landscape" that cannot be screened away. §5.15 · §11

This is the admission that unlocks the override, and it is also useful on its own: it is the SA proving your central theme in its own voice, that a heavy industrial plant does not belong in this rural, agricultural place. Every argument about farmland, noise, fire, and local bans is downstream of that same truth. Quote it.

Part III
The legal attack

15The "mitigated" magic trick

The doctrine that ties it together

Step back and one move repeats across the sixteen categories: name a real impact, attach a condition of certification, declare it solved. And the conditions are, overwhelmingly, plans to be written after approval.

A Fire Prevention Plan. A soil-and-water sampling plan. A Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan. A construction management plan. An agricultural easement to be located later. A post-fire monitoring plan. This is the defect CEQA calls improperly deferred mitigation: an agency may not find an impact "less than significant" today by promising to devise the solution tomorrow. California courts (e.g., Sundstrom, Communities for a Better Environment) have repeatedly invalidated approvals that substitute "we will make a plan" for present analysis and enforceable performance standards.

The diagnostic question, applied to every "with implementation of COC [X], impacts would be less than significant" sentence in the document, is the same: does the SA show the measure will actually work, with an enforceable performance standard and independent verification, or does it merely require that a plan be produced? Where it is the latter, the significance finding is an assertion, not a conclusion supported by substantial evidence. Catalog enough of these and the whole "fifteen of sixteen are fine" edifice weakens at once.

16Contesting the override

The final gate

Because visual impact is significant and unavoidable, the CEC can only approve by adopting a statement of overriding considerations under CEQA Guidelines §§15091 and 15093, a formal finding that the project's benefits outweigh the unavoidable harm, supported by substantial evidence. §11

The SA's benefits recital is the standard clean-energy boilerplate: grid reliability, jobs, help meeting SB 100 / SB 1020 renewable targets, displacement of fossil generation. Two lines of attack:

  • The benefits are generic, the harms are specific. Nothing in the benefits list is unique to this site, the same 300 MW of storage delivers identical grid benefits at Lambie Industrial Park, without the farmland loss or the local-ban override. When the benefits are location-independent but the harms are location-specific, the override balance is weak, and it loops straight back to the alternatives failure in §5.
  • The record must be clean. An override rests on the adequacy of the underlying EIR. If the alternatives analysis is inadequate and multiple significance findings rest on deferred mitigation, the foundation for the override is compromised, you attack the balance and the evidence beneath it.
Kill-shot question

"Every benefit cited for the override, grid reliability, jobs, renewable targets, would be delivered equally by the same project at the environmentally superior Lambie site. How can benefits that are not site-specific outweigh harms that are, and how can an override stand on an alternatives analysis staff admit was less detailed for the superior option?"

17Your procedural rights

How to be heard where it counts

The Staff Assessment is a draft. There is a public comment period, and typically an evidentiary or public hearing, before the Commission decides. What you file now becomes the administrative record, and a court reviewing a later challenge is generally limited to that record. Practical implications:

  • Exhaustion. Issues not raised during the administrative process usually cannot be raised in court later. Every argument in this brief must be submitted as a comment to preserve it. Silence now forfeits it.
  • Evidence beats opinion. Expert declarations, an independent biologist on the creek/canal, an energy-CEQA attorney on deferred mitigation and alternatives, carry far more weight in the record than lay comment, and they are what a judge later reads.
  • Standing and allies. The County and City each had their ordinance overridden and have standing and counsel; a local government objecting to a state override is powerful. Recruit them, and confirm whether formal intervenor status is available and worthwhile under the Opt-In rules.
  • Deadlines are hard. Confirm the comment deadline and hearing dates in the live docket immediately; Opt-In timelines are compressed and unforgiving.
Part IV
Action

18The playbook

In order of leverage:

  1. Get on the docket now

    File written comments in CEC Docket 24-OPT-05 via the state e-filing system. Confirm the current comment deadline and hearing dates first, everything here must land on the record to be preserved.

  2. Lead with alternatives and the two bans

    Your hardest legal point (inadequate alternatives) and your hardest political point (two overridden democratic votes). Both travel to a courtroom and a newspaper equally well.

  3. Recruit the County and City

    Both had ordinances overridden; both have standing and lawyers; the Board already floated Lambie. A local government fighting a state override is the ally no citizen group can substitute for.

  4. Retain two experts

    An independent biologist (creek/canal/wetlands framing, frac-out, firewater to downstream users) and an energy-CEQA attorney (deferred mitigation, alternatives, the override). Reuse whoever won San Juan Capistrano.

  5. Make two specific demands

    A funded, inflation-indexed decommissioning bond, and a real CDFW §1602 agreement instead of the CEC's self-issued "equivalency." Concrete, reasonable, and revealing when resisted.

  6. Own the public narrative

    Hydrogen fluoride and cyanide, 880 feet from homes, on farmland the community voted to protect, cleared by the state after two local bans. Moss Landing is the reference everyone already knows.

19Comment-letter skeleton

A structure your attorney can flesh out and file on Docket 24-OPT-05. Each heading maps to a section above.

List each "less than significant with mitigation" finding whose COC is merely a future plan lacking enforceable performance standards, fire, firewater, stormwater, farmland easement, frac-out. §15

4 · Fire, toxic gas, and public health are understated

Significant fire risk reduced by training alone; the school "ruler" answer; pre-Moss-Landing evidence; the fire scenario excluded from the public-health significance finding. §6, §9

5 · Water & wildlife harms to downstream users are unanalyzed

Frac-out and firewater pathways to the SID canal and shallow groundwater; no downstream-user analysis; the CEC's streambed "equivalency" should be a CDFW §1602 agreement. §7

6 · Farmland & cumulative impacts

Off-site easement is not real mitigation; cumulative farmland/fire/water impacts with the second nearby BESS, California Forever, and planned housing are inadequately analyzed. §8, §13

7 · Decommissioning lacks financial assurance

Single-purpose LLC; no funded bond; condition any approval on an inflation-indexed decommissioning surety. §12

8 · The override is unsupported

Generic benefits cannot outweigh site-specific harms available impact-free at the superior alternative; the override rests on a compromised record. §16

9 · Requested relief

Deny certification; or at minimum recirculate a revised assessment with an adequate alternatives analysis, enforceable (non-deferred) mitigation, a decommissioning bond, and a real CDFW agreement.
