When the Community Said No: Three Years of Civic Advocacy in San Juan Capistrano

How a proposed industrial battery storage facility near residential neighborhoods sparked a sustained civic campaign — and what happened when a community came together with research, expertise, and resolve.

Back to Insights

A few years ago, a proposal surfaced to build a large Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) near residential neighborhoods in San Juan Capistrano. At first glance, it sounded like a routine infrastructure project — the kind that gets approved quietly in a planning meeting most people never attend. But as I began looking into the details, it became clear that this was anything but routine.

The proposed site was 29251 Camino Capistrano — a 15-22 acre stretch of land at the main entrance to the city, bordering Oso Creek, flanked by the communities of San Juan Capistrano and Laguna Niguel, and home to the Saddleback Church PEACE Farm: a 100% volunteer-run, non-profit agricultural facility that had provided over 150 tons of free, locally-grown produce to families in need since 2018.

The developer, Broad Reach Power, LLC (later acquired by French utility giant ENGIE), proposed replacing the PEACE Farm with several hundred shipping containers of lithium-ion batteries, a switchyard, substation, and two new electrical transmission towers. When the San Juan Capistrano City Council denied the initial rezone application in November 2022, the developer signaled they would appeal directly to the State of California — effectively bypassing the community's decision entirely.

That's when I decided to get involved.

Applying Professional Skills to a Civic Problem

My professional background is in government consulting. I've spent my career supporting federal agencies — NASA, the U.S. Department of the Interior, the Federal Highway Administration — on internal controls, risk management, public policy analysis, and stakeholder engagement. I know what it looks like to evaluate complex systems under scrutiny. I know how to read a project impact, identify where the risks are, and communicate findings clearly to decision-makers.

I applied that same framework here.

I conducted an independent project impact study and authored a detailed report outlining the safety, environmental, fiscal, and community concerns associated with placing a 250 MW lithium-ion battery storage facility in close proximity to residential communities and ecologically sensitive land. The findings were significant — and they needed to be heard.

The Safety Case: Not Just a Local Concern

Battery energy storage systems play a real and growing role in the transition to modern energy infrastructure. That's not in dispute. But location matters enormously — and this location raised serious red flags.

The site falls within a Moderate to High fire risk zone as classified by the Orange County Fire Authority. Lithium-ion batteries are susceptible to a process called thermal runaway: an uncontrolled cascade of exothermic reactions that produces flammable gases, extreme heat, and the potential for explosion. According to the BESS Fire Event Database, there had been 68 documented BESS fire incidents globally since 2011 — 11 in 2023 alone, 8 of them in the United States.

Lithium-ion battery fires are notoriously hard to extinguish because they burn at extremely high temperatures and produce dangerous fumes.

— The Wall Street Journal, September 22, 2022

The scale of a utility-grade BESS is orders of magnitude larger than a consumer battery. The risks are proportionally greater. And the proposed location — dry grassland, native brush, a creek bed, surrounded by residential neighborhoods — created a combination that demanded careful public scrutiny.

Environmental Stakes: The Creek, the Wildlife, the Land

The ecological concerns extended well beyond fire risk. Oso Creek runs directly through the project area before flowing into San Juan Creek and ultimately draining to Doheny State Beach and Dana Point Harbor. A chemical leak — which can occur from manufacturing defects, physical damage, or temperature exposure — would introduce toxic "forever chemicals" into a watershed that flows through multiple municipalities and into a protected coastal estuary.

The site itself is identified by the Sea and Sage Audubon Society of Orange County as a natural habitat for rare migratory and native birds. Among the species documented in the area: the Coastal California Gnatcatcher, Burrowing Owl, White-Tailed Kite, Cooper's Hawk (listed on the CDFW Watch List), and the endangered Steelhead Trout in the adjacent creek. The project footprint — 15 to 22 acres of grading that would move approximately 324,000 cubic yards of soil — would permanently disrupt habitat that took generations to establish.

San Juan Capistrano is also a city with 43 documented prehistoric archaeological sites within city limits. City Council Policy 601 requires archaeological and Native American monitoring for any project with soil disturbance greater than 18 inches. The proposed project would disturb far more than that — on land that sits within a culturally sensitive area. That kind of irreversible impact deserves more than a footnote in a developer's application.

Community Impact: More Than a View

Residents who purchased homes in San Juan Capistrano and Laguna Niguel did so with the expectation of a certain quality of life — including the natural landscapes that have defined this area for over 220 years. The proposed facility would have been the first thing people saw driving into the city off the I-5. It would have blocked community hiking and biking trails. It would have raised homeowner insurance premiums — or canceled policies outright, as was already happening in neighboring San Clemente with a similar BESS project nearby.

The PEACE Farm — which had become a cornerstone of the local community, feeding hundreds of families at no cost — would have been displaced entirely. There is no easy way to relocate a working farm. Crops die. Community ties fray. Years of volunteer labor don't simply transfer to a new address.

Three Years in the Room

What began as research became a campaign. Over three years, I delivered public testimony at:

  • Three San Juan Capistrano City Council meetings
  • An Orange County meeting
  • Two South Orange County Community College District board meetings
  • Two California Energy Commission proceedings

I also served as a keynote speaker and organizer for seven community forums across San Juan Capistrano and Laguna Niguel, helping residents understand the proposal and engage constructively in the formal public process. I submitted written comments and letters to the California Energy Commission documenting safety and zoning considerations for the official regulatory record. And I briefly served as a founding board member of BLESSIN, a nonprofit established to support structured community engagement and advocacy around responsible development and public safety.

The goal throughout was never to stop energy infrastructure categorically. It was to ask a simple question: Is this the right place for this project?

What Changed

After three years of sustained civic engagement, the effort helped produce a series of meaningful outcomes:

  • The City of San Juan Capistrano enacted a moratorium on battery energy storage projects while additional safety considerations were evaluated.
  • The South Orange County Community College District reversed its prior support for the project following sustained community review.
  • Orange County began exploring similar protective measures at the regional level.
  • ENGIE withdrew its request to move forward with the proposed facility at that location.

The PEACE Farm remains. The creek still flows. The trails are still open.

What This Taught Me

This experience reinforced something I've seen in every high-stakes consulting engagement I've been part of: process matters, but so does the people doing it. The most rigorous risk assessment in the world only creates change if someone is willing to put it in front of the right audience, at the right time, in the right format.

Civic advocacy is, in many ways, a form of stakeholder engagement. You identify the decision-makers. You understand their constraints. You build a factual, well-documented case. You show up — repeatedly — and you make it easy for leaders to make the right call.

I also believe that civic participation is a responsibility. My early experiences as a California Girls State Representative in 2014 and as a member of the American Legion Women's Auxiliary gave me a foundational understanding of how public institutions work and why they need informed, engaged citizens to function well. This campaign was an extension of that same belief.

Progress matters. Innovation matters. So does thoughtful planning. Responsible development means ensuring that infrastructure moves forward in ways that protect the people and places it's meant to serve. Sometimes the most meaningful outcome of a civic process is the one where a community asks the right questions — and gets heard.
